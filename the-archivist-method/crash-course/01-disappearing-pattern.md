Someone gets close. You leave.

Not always physically. Sometimes you go quiet. You stop initiating. You become unavailable. You find reasons the relationship is wrong. You manufacture an exit. But you leave. Every time. Right when it starts to matter.

Notice what your body just did while reading that.

The chest tightened. Or the throat did. Or the stomach dropped, the way it drops in an elevator that started moving before you were ready. The body recognized the pattern faster than the mind did. The body always does.

The pattern has a name. The Disappearing Pattern. It is one of the nine. It runs every time someone gets close enough to matter, every time the relationship crosses from casual to real, every time the door of the room you are in starts to feel less like a door and more like a wall. You do not feel like you are leaving. You feel like you are protecting yourself from a danger you cannot quite name.

The danger has a name. The threshold.

Below the threshold, you are fine. Warm. Connected. Even loving. The first weeks are convincing. The first months are convincing. You pursue the closeness. You want it. You are good at it, at low doses. Then someone says the wrong true thing, the thing that makes the connection real, and the threshold crosses, and the floor drops, and the pattern fires.

Within hours, the cancellations begin. Within weeks, the response times double. Within months, the relationship is over and you call it a mismatch. The mismatch was not the relationship. The mismatch was the threshold being crossed in a body that has not been told the danger is over.

From the outside, you are the person who cannot commit. Who always has one foot out the door. Who is amazing for the first three months and then slowly, or suddenly, vanishes. Your partners describe you as hot and cold. Emotionally unavailable. A runner. A ghost.

From the inside, it does not feel like running. It feels like survival. The relationship starts to feel heavy. Suffocating. Like a hand around your throat. You are not leaving because you do not care. You are leaving because the closeness activated a survival response and the body is screaming to find the exit, and the body does not know that the exit is also where the loneliness lives.

The cruelest part: you want the closeness. You crave it. You pursue it. And then when it arrives, the program activates and destroys it. You build the connection. You burn it down. You watch yourself do this and cannot stop.

The pattern is designed to protect you from being abandoned. It does this by leaving first. If you leave, you cannot be left. If you never get close, you cannot lose anyone. The result: you are always alone. The thing the pattern was built to prevent is the thing the pattern guarantees. Every relationship you left to protect yourself from pain produced the exact pain you were avoiding. You just controlled the timing.

## 2. MECHANISM

Forty milliseconds. That is how long the amygdala takes to read the word "love" as a threat.

A signal arrives. An intimacy signal. The auditory cortex registers the words "I love you." The visual cortex registers the face moving closer, the hand reaching, the body angling toward yours. The signal routes to the thalamus, which forwards it in parallel to two destinations. The slow path runs through the prefrontal cortex, where conscious evaluation happens, in three to five hundred milliseconds. The fast path runs straight to the amygdala, in forty to fifty. The amygdala does not wait. It checks the signal against stored threat patterns. If a match exists, the sympathetic nervous system fires before the conscious mind has registered what was said.

The diaphragm locks. Cortisol releases. Blood shunts from the extremities toward the large muscles of the legs. The chest band, the cold hands, the urge to move, all downstream of that amygdala fire. By the time the conscious mind thinks "I should stay," the body has been moving toward the door for three full seconds.

Why this circuit fires for love.

In the Original Room, the room where the pattern was installed, closeness preceded loss. The caregiver who got close was the caregiver who left, or who was unpredictable, or who absorbed too much of the child's attention before withdrawing. Sometimes the loss was literal. A parent died, divorced, disappeared, deployed, drank. Sometimes the loss was emotional. The caregiver was physically present and emotionally absent, or present in good moods and absent in bad ones, or close one day and unreachable the next. The lesson the body encoded: closeness is the precondition for loss. The closeness is what makes the loss possible. Avoid closeness, avoid loss.

The amygdala does not check whether the original room is the current room. It fires the same alarm in the new room as the old. The intimacy signal arrives. The amygdala registers the threshold being approached. The threshold is not a quantity of love. It is the specific moment when closeness in the present becomes recognizable as the same shape as closeness in the past. The shape is what the amygdala matches. The match fires. The flight begins.

This is a flight response. Fight, flight, freeze, fawn, the four ancient survival outputs of the brainstem. The Disappearing Pattern is the flight one. It is the fastest of the four because flight is the most physically active. The body does not need to think. The body needs to move.

The relief that follows the disappearance is dopamine. Same neurochemistry as any successful flight from threat. The chest loosens. The breath returns. The room feels right-sized. The body logs: distance equals safety. Closeness equals danger. Disappear equals safe. Every disappearance strengthens the circuit. Every relief reinforces it. The pattern is not weak. It is well-rehearsed and biologically rewarded.

The pattern is reinforced socially in a way the other patterns are not. Culture rewards the cool. Culture rewards the one who is not too eager. Culture has whole vocabularies for staying detached and almost none for the work of staying close. The reward signal layers on top of the dopamine: every disappearance produces inner relief and outer respect. The pattern is fed from both sides.

The pattern math is always wrong. The automatic thoughts ("they will leave eventually," "this will not work," "I am protecting them from getting hurt") inflate the certainty of future loss and minimize the cost of preempting it. The math decides for you what is true and the answer is always: leave now. The math is the amygdala thinking out loud.

## 3. BODY SIGNATURE

The diaphragm goes first.

It locks before you have heard the full sentence. The breath stops being available. The next inhale is shallower than the one before it. By the time you have processed the words that arrived, the chest is already a band, and the band is already tightening. The body knows the threshold has been crossed. The body always does.

The first signal is the chest band. A specific tightness around the rib cage, like something has been wrapped one notch too tight just under the sternum. Not the panic-attack chest. Not the heart-attack chest. The flight chest. It activates within two seconds of the intimacy signal landing and stays until distance is restored. Some people describe it as a band. Some describe it as a hand. Either way, the body is preparing for departure.

The second signal is the claustrophobia. The room shrinks. The other person feels too close even when they are across the table. The walls move inward by an inch you cannot measure but can feel. The space is not the space. The space is your body's perception of the space, and your body is reading the room as a container that is closing.

The third signal is the eye scan. Doors. Windows. Phone. Keys. Bag. Exits. The eyes start mapping escape routes within seconds, before the mind has consciously decided that escape is the plan. You will catch yourself glancing at the door of a room you entered willingly. You will catch yourself reaching for your phone for no message. The body is looking for the exit while the mouth is still saying nice things.

The fourth signal is the urge to move. The legs want to stand. The feet shift. The body cannot stay seated for the conversation it is in. There is no specific somewhere to go. There is only away. The urge will dress itself up as a reasonable need. A bathroom. A glass of water. A thing remembered that needs handling right now. The reasonable need is the pattern's costume.

The fifth signal is the cool hands. Blood shunts away from the extremities and toward the large muscles. The fingertips go cool. The palms can go cool. This is preparation. The body is allocating resources for the run. Cool hands during a love conversation are diagnostic.

The sixth signal is the stomach drop. Not nausea. A drop. The way the floor of an elevator drops when the cable catches. The moment someone says "I love you" or "What are we" or "Move in with me," the floor drops. This is the threshold being crossed registered as a specific physical event. You will feel it in the gut before you process it in the head.

The seventh signal is the face. A small flatness. The expression pulls back. The face stops showing what you feel because what you feel is the wrong feeling for the moment. People watching you may notice the change before you do.

You will not have all seven. You will probably have three or four, in a recognizable order, every time. The order is your signature. Once you know it, the pattern stops being subtle. The diaphragm locks, the eyes scan, the hands go cool, and you know what is about to happen before it happens. That gap is where the protocol lives.
