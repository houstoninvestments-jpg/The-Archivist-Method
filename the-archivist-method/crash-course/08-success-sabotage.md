The pattern fires when things are working.

Not when they fail. When they work. When the relationship deepens. When the savings cross a threshold. When the project lands. When the doctor says good news. When the calm holds for long enough that you start to relax into it. That is the moment the body reaches for the match.

You know this about yourself. You have known it for years. You have watched yourself do it. You have watched your hand reach for the thing that will burn it down, and you have watched yourself send the message anyway, miss the deadline anyway, pick the fight anyway. Awareness alone has not been enough.

Notice what your body did while reading that.

The chest got tighter. Or the skin got restless. Or a specific moment came into focus, the moment the email went out or the affair started or the deadline slipped, and the thought of it produced a small flicker the body recognized. The body recognized the pattern faster than the mind did. The body always does.

The pattern has a name. Success Sabotage. It is one of the nine. It runs every time stability arrives in a body that has been calibrated for chaos, every time the savings reach a number the body has not held before, every time the relationship enters a depth the body has not stayed inside before. You do not feel like you are sabotaging. You feel like you are bored. Or restless. Or that something is off.

What the body is feeling has a name. The set point.

Your nervous system has a learned baseline. The baseline was set in a room where stability was always followed by disruption, where calm was the precursor to crisis, where good things had a half-life. The baseline encoded that half-life as your normal hormonal load. When current life produces sustained calm, your stress hormones drop below the baseline, and the body reads the drop as deviation. Restlessness rises. Agitation rises. The body wants the familiar load back. The match is the fastest way to get it.

From the outside, you are the person who blows it up at the worst possible moment. Who self-destructs. Who has a track record of brilliant starts and devastating finishes. Friends and partners stop being surprised. They have seen the script. They watch you walk yourself toward the match, and they have learned not to ask you to put it down because you cannot hear them.

From the inside, the destruction does not feel destructive in the moment. It feels like correction. Like restoring something. Like getting it over with. The pattern dresses up in costumes, boredom, dissatisfaction, "needing a change," "not feeling it anymore," and each costume gives you permission to act on the impulse that is actually about returning your body to its calibrated state.

The cruelest part: when the consequences arrive, the pattern points at the wreckage and says good things never last. You nod. You believe it. You forget that you were the one who lit the match.

## 2. MECHANISM

Cortisol below baseline. That is what the body is feeling.

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, the body's stress-hormone chain, has a learned set point. The set point is a chronic level of stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline, norepinephrine) that the body considers normal. The set point is not chosen. It is calibrated by repeated experience, especially in childhood, when the nervous system was still writing the rules.

If you grew up in cyclical chaos, the set point is high. The body learned to operate at high cortisol because high cortisol was the daily climate. Sustained calm, as an adult, drops the hormones below the learned set point. The body reads the drop the way a thermostat reads a falling temperature. It generates compensatory arousal. The arousal feels like restlessness, agitation, skin-crawling, an unnamed anxiety that scans the room for a threat and finds none.

The body is not detecting a threat. It is correcting an imbalance toward the chaotic baseline it was calibrated to. The arousal is the correction in process. The arousal is what the pattern uses to drive you toward the match.

When you light the match, when the fight starts or the email sends or the money goes, the act produces a surge of adrenaline. The adrenaline spikes the cortisol back toward the learned set point. The body returns to its familiar hormonal profile. The pressure releases. The unbearable calm is replaced by familiar chaos. Relief is chemical, not evidentiary. The mind arrives last and writes the cover story: I was bored. I needed a change. They did not really know me.

The cover story is the pattern in costume.

Why this circuit fires for stability.

In the Original Room, the room where the pattern was installed, calm was the precursor to crisis. The good day preceded the bad night. The peaceful weekend preceded the screaming Monday. The praise preceded the punishment. The body learned the pattern not as a thought but as a hormonal sequence. Calm equals warning. Stability equals imminent loss. Sustained good equals countdown to disaster. The countdown was always running. The disaster always landed.

The amygdala does not check whether the original room is the current room. It fires the same alarm in the new room as the old. Sustained good arrives. The amygdala registers the absence of crisis as the precursor to crisis. The body is not waiting for something bad to happen. The body is preparing the bad thing itself, on schedule, on its own terms, before the universe can deliver it.

The relief that follows the destruction is dopamine and the adrenaline crash combined. Same neurochemistry as any successful threat resolution. The pressure releases. The room feels right-sized again. The body logs: chaos equals familiar, sabotage equals safe. Every sabotage strengthens the circuit. Every relief reinforces it. The pattern is not weak. The pattern math is always wrong. The automatic thoughts ("this cannot last," "something is about to go wrong," "might as well get it over with") inflate the certainty of future loss and minimize the cost of preempting it. The math decides for you what is true and the answer is always: act now. The math is the set point thinking out loud.

## 3. BODY SIGNATURE

The body itches when nothing is wrong.

You will not name it as itching at first. You will call it boredom. Restlessness. A vague feeling that something is off. None of those names are accurate. The body is producing a discomfort that has no external cause, in response to a hormonal drop your conscious mind cannot detect. By the time you have given the discomfort a name, the pattern has already begun building a story to make the name make sense.

The first signal is the restlessness. Not the restlessness of a long meeting. The restlessness of sustained calm. You cannot sit still in a room where everything is fine. The body is generating motion because the still moment registers as wrong. The legs bounce. The hands fidget. The mind reaches for a problem and finds none, and the not-finding makes the restlessness worse.

The second signal is the skin-crawl. A surface-level agitation, a sense that something is moving on the skin or under the skin or just past the skin, that has no source. The skin-crawl arrives during peace. It worsens during contentment. It is the most diagnostic marker of this pattern because it does not arrive during any other state. If you feel the skin-crawl while everything is going right, the pattern is firing.

The third signal is the chest pressure. Not the chest band of the Disappearing Pattern. Not the flutter of the Testing Pattern. A diffuse pressure, like a hand on the sternum that is not heavy enough to alarm and not light enough to ignore. The chest pressure rises in proportion to how good things are going. The better the day, the heavier the pressure.

The fourth signal is the disrupted sleep. You wake at three in the morning. Not because you are anxious about anything. Because you are not anxious about anything. The body has spent the day at low cortisol and is correcting in the night, generating arousal that interrupts sleep, scanning the dark room for the threat that justifies the arousal.

The fifth signal is the heightened startle. Sounds make you jump. A door closes and your shoulders fly up. The body is in low-grade hypervigilance because the calm is reading as the precursor to a sudden bad event. The startle is the body waiting for the bad event to arrive.

The sixth signal is the appetite shift. You overeat when life is stable. Or undereat. Either way, the relationship to food becomes uneven during peace, after months of being normal during stress. The body is using food to spike or suppress arousal. The cortisol axis routes through hunger.

The seventh signal is the unnamed anxiety. The signature feeling. Something is wrong. You scan your environment for the threat. You check your phone for bad news. You audit the relationship for cracks. You search the project for mistakes. You find nothing. The not-finding makes the anxiety worse, because the body is certain something is wrong, and the absence of evidence does not change the certainty.

You will not have all seven. You will probably have three or four, in a recognizable order, every time the calm holds. The order is your signature. Once you know it, the pattern stops being subtle. The skin starts to crawl, the chest pressures, the unnamed anxiety arrives, and you know what is about to happen before it happens. That gap is where the protocol lives.
