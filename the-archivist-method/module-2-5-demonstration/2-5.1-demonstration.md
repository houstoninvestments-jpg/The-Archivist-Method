# 2-5.1 DEMONSTRATION

The Four Doors are a framework. Frameworks read flat on the page. To make the framework real, run it on something specific. Something small. Something you have probably done in the last two hours.

Pick: the phone-check when you should be working.

You sit down to do the thing. The proposal. The chapter. The email that needs writing. You open the document. You write seven words. Your hand reaches for your phone. You check it. You scroll for ninety seconds. You return to the document. You write three more words. Your hand reaches again.

This is a small pattern. It is not one of the nine patterns. No one is going to lose their marriage to it. But it costs you the project, the deadline, the afternoon. And it operates on the same circuit as every pattern in this book: trigger, body signature, gap, behavior, relief, reinforcement.

Watch the framework run on it.

---

## DOOR 1: FOCUS

The first door is labeling. You catch the pattern while it is happening.

The work begins. Resistance arrives. Your chest tightens slightly. Your hand begins moving. You are about to check the phone.

Label it: "The avoidance circuit just fired."

Not "I am bad at focus." Not "I have ADHD." Not "I just need a break." Those are stories. The label is mechanical: a circuit fired. Your hand moved. The program is running.

The labeling does not stop the phone-check the first time. It does not need to. Door 1 is sight, not action. You see the pattern. You do not judge it. You name what you see.

---

## DOOR 2: EXCAVATION

The second door asks where the circuit came from. For full patterns this is a long excavation. For this small pattern, give it sixty seconds.

The avoidance circuit installs whenever effort produced punishment, withdrawal, or shame. The child sat down to draw. The drawing was not good enough. The child learned: sustained effort exposes you. Discontinuous effort, distracted effort, half-hearted effort, all of these produce plausible deniability if the work fails. You did not really try. So you did not really fail.

Sixty seconds. That is enough. The point of Excavation is not to fix the original room. The point is to know that the circuit is older than this afternoon and it is not a moral failure.

Move on.

---

## DOOR 3: INTERRUPTION

The third door is the script. You speak it out loud, in the gap, before the hand reaches.

Script: "The phone is the pattern. I am writing for fifteen minutes. The phone is in the next room."

Three sentences. You say them. Then you stand up. You take the phone to the next room. You put it on a shelf. You close the door. You sit back down.

The interruption is not internal. It is environmental. The pattern relies on the phone being within reach. Move the phone. The circuit is broken at the hardware level.

You will feel the urge to retrieve it. The urge peaks at about forty seconds. It fades by ninety seconds if you do not act on it.

---

## DOOR 4: REWRITE

The fourth door installs a new default. The avoidance circuit was meeting a real need: relief from the discomfort of sustained focus. The Rewrite has to meet the same need without the cost.

Replacement: every fifteen minutes of work, two minutes of intentional rest. Stand up. Drink water. Look out a window. The rest is scheduled. It is not a reaction to discomfort. It is a planned interval.

The rest meets the need that the phone-check was meeting. But the rest does not fragment the work. It interrupts it on a schedule and then ends.

Run this for one afternoon. The circuit weakens. Run it for a week. The new default starts to compete with the old one.

---

## NOW

That was the framework on a small pattern.

You watched the Four Doors operate. Focus saw the circuit. Excavation named where it came from. Interruption broke it in the gap. Rewrite replaced it with something that met the same need at lower cost.

The patterns in Module 3 are larger. The stakes are higher. The architecture is the same.

Now we run this on the patterns that matter.
